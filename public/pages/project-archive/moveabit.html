Move A Bit

Move a Bit is a live motion capture project bringing quantum computing to life through an interactive display that visually showcases entanglement.

Collaborators: Isabella Wang, Youn Hwang, Kalinda Panholzer and Si Ran Pang

Role: Concept, Backend Developer, Quantum Computing, Connecting to Touchdesigner, Experience design

Quantum Computing | Touchdesigner | 2022

Introduction

This project was created during the Quantum Design Jam at The New School with IBM. The project brings the concepts of quantum computing to life through a fun and interactive experience. It visually showcases the concept of entanglement through the interaction of the viewer with the system. Just as a special correlation exists between qubits, the particles in Move a Bit have a special relationship with the subject in motion. Quibit is the basic unit in quantum computing into which quantum information is stored/ encoded. In this project, movement produces the quantum state with all quantum possibilities represented in different colors. Stillness represents the observed collapsed, classical state.We are entangled with the system.


Generating quantum data

To generate the quantum data to guide the properties of our motion reactive particles, we used the W-state to entangle three quibits. W-state is a means of entangling 3 quibits robustly especially when a quibit is lost. The quantum circuit was composed of reset, RY, CU and controlled-x gates. The circuit also had three measurement info or z-basis at the end to measure the quibit values in the classical register. We chose to work with 3 quibits because we wanted the quantum data to guide the RGB color values of our particles.

Generating particles

The quantum data was then put into touchdesigner: a node based visual programming language for real-time interactive content. The data had to first be formatted so that it could be put into the RGB ramp in touchdesigner to control the colour of the quantum particles. Since the values of the RGB columns were entangled, it created a limited color palette. The particles that aren’t of the original colors are a demonstration of noise, or quantum computation errors. Essentially as you move, the particles of the quantum system move with you. The project only exists in correlation to the viewer.

Exhibition

Exhibited at the Microscope Gallery for the Quantum Art Exhibition organized by IBM Quantum and Parsons School of Design in March 2023.
